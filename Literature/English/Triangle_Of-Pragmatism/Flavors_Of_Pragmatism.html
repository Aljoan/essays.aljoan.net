The Triangle of Pragmatism: Justice, Stability, Resistance

(the Humanist/Intellectual version)

By: Qais Aljoan - September 2025

“Pragmatism,” they say, as if it were a single road. But there are many streets with that name, and some of them end at the sea...

Fifteen Flavors of Pragmatism: Philosophies, Pillars, and Practitioners

By Qais Aljoan — 2025

Introduction

Pragmatism is not a single doctrine. In the Palestine–Israel conflict, it has become a kaleidoscope of strategies, each claiming to be the sober road between despair and illusion. Yet each flavor of pragmatism has roots in a philosophy, pillars that sustain it, and adepts who carry it forward. What follows is not a catalogue of policies, but an anatomy of fifteen currents, each with its genealogy and its disciples.

1. Technocratic Pragmatism

Born in the 1990s under the shadow of the Oslo Accords, technocratic pragmatism treats politics as management. Its creed is logistics, budgets, and checkpoints disguised as “coordination.” The guiding philosophy is that survival can be administered without confronting the structures that deny freedom.

Adepts: Salam Fayyad (PA Prime Minister, 2007–2013),1 James Wolfensohn (World Bank President, 1995–2005),2 Mahmoud Abbas during his state-building phase,3 Tony Blair as Quartet envoy,4 and successive UNRWA commissioners-general.5

2. Ethical–Legal Pragmatism

This current insists that law is not ornament but compass. Its origins lie in the UN Charter, the Geneva Conventions, and the anti-apartheid legal campaigns of the 1970s. It holds that peace without rights is illusion.

Adepts: Francesca Albanese (UN Special Rapporteur on Palestine),6 John Dugard (South African jurist, former UN SR),7 Richard Falk (UN SR 2008–2014),8 Raji Sourani (founder, Palestinian Centre for Human Rights),9 and Shawan Jabarin (Al-Haq director).10

3. Security Pragmatism

Security pragmatism views politics through intelligence files and border maps. It emerged from Egypt’s 1979 treaty, Jordan’s 1994 treaty, and decades of coordination with Israeli agencies. It privileges regime survival and short-term calm over rights.

Adepts: Mohammed Dahlan (PA security chief),11 Jibril Rajoub (PA intelligence figure),12 Omar Suleiman (Egyptian intelligence chief),13 King Abdullah II of Jordan,14 and Amos Gilad (Israeli defense strategist).15

That centroid (-5, +9, -8) works well for a single Security Pragmatism bucket, even though the actors aren’t homogeneous.

Why this vector makes sense

  • Justice = –5: Security-first actors routinely subordinate rights/accountability to de-escalation, coordination, and “manageability.” They’ll cite law when convenient, but enforcement that risks instability is deferred.
  • Stability = +9: This is the core: ceasefires, calm, border control, intelligence cooperation, and preventing spillover. Nearly everything is optimized for near-term order.
  • Resistance = –8: The logic is to suppress armed or disruptive resistance (raids, arrests, bans, kinetic deterrence). Even civil resistance that threatens “order” tends to be contained.

Tolerance band (optional for error bars)

  • Justice: ~[–7, –3] (e.g., some U.S./EU process managers hover closer to –3; harder-line security chiefs nearer –7).
  • Stability: ~[+8, +10] (this barely moves—stability is the job).
  • Resistance: ~[–9, –6] (variation by posture and moment; crisis periods push toward –9).

Practical use

  • Use (–5, +9, –8) as the canonical centroid in the visuals.
  • If you want nuance, plot sub-points for:
    • PA Security (e.g., Faraj/Rajoub): (–6, +9, –8)
    • Israel Defense/Intel (e.g., Gallant/Halevi/Shin Bet): (–6, +9, –9)
    • Egypt/Jordan brokers (e.g., Kamel/Abdullah II): (–4, +10, –7)
    • U.S. process managers (e.g., Burns/McGurk): (–4, +9, –7)

4. Resistance Pragmatism

Resistance pragmatism holds that without deterrence, negotiations are submission. It draws on anti-colonial struggles from Algeria’s FLN to South Africa’s ANC. The creed: force is leverage, but it must be disciplined.

Adepts: Sheikh Ahmed Yassin (Hamas founder),16 Ramadan Shallah (Palestinian Islamic Jihad leader),17 Hassan Nasrallah (Hizbullah secretary-general),18 Khaled Meshaal (Hamas political bureau),19 and George Habash (PFLP founder).20

5. Diaspora–Integration Pragmatism

This current works in classrooms, media, and parliaments abroad. Its roots lie in Jewish diaspora lobbying pre-1948 and in the anti-apartheid campaigns in London and Washington. It sees the long arc of change through syllabi, elections, and culture.

Adepts: Edward Said (Columbia University scholar),21 Rashida Tlaib (U.S. Congresswoman),22 Ilhan Omar (U.S. Congresswoman),23 Hanan Ashrawi (Palestinian negotiator),24 and Susan Abulhawa (Palestinian novelist and activist).25

6. Gulf–Normalization Pragmatism

This pragmatism trades moral clarity for commercial dividends. Its roots stretch from Sadat’s Camp David to the Abraham Accords. The philosophy: recognition and trade will pacify conflicts better than boycotts.

Adepts: Mohammed bin Zayed (UAE),26 Jared Kushner (Abraham Accords architect),27 Anwar Gargash (UAE diplomat),28 Abdullatif bin Rashid Al Zayani (Bahrain FM),29 and Mohammed bin Salman (Saudi crown prince, exploratory normalization).30

7. U.S. Strategic Pragmatism

For Washington, pragmatism is code for hegemony managed through Israel’s security. Its genealogy runs from Truman’s 1948 recognition to Kissinger’s shuttle diplomacy and Biden’s 2020s strategy.

Adepts: Harry Truman (recognized Israel in 1948),31 Henry Kissinger (U.S. Secretary of State, 1973–77),32 Joe Biden,33 Antony Blinken (U.S. Secretary of State),34 and Dennis Ross (peace process envoy).35

8. European Conditional Pragmatism

Europe preaches law but practices exceptions. From the EU’s labeling of settlement goods to Macron’s recognition with conditions, Europe’s pragmatism is recognition tied to caveats.

Adepts: Emmanuel Macron (President of France),36 Josep Borrell (EU High Representative),37 Federica Mogherini (former EU HR),38 Angela Merkel (German Chancellor, 2005–21),39 and Javier Solana (EU foreign policy chief, 1999–2009).40

9. Russian/Chinese Pragmatism

Palestine here is a pawn in a multipolar board. The Soviet Union once armed liberation movements; today, Russia and China invoke Palestine as leverage against U.S. primacy.

Adepts: Vladimir Putin,41 Sergey Lavrov (Russian FM),42 Xi Jinping,43 Wang Yi (Chinese FM),44 and Yevgeny Primakov (Russian strategist).45

10. Islamic-World Pragmatism

Split between confrontational and transactional camps, this pragmatism is draped in religious legitimacy but driven by regional rivalry. The OIC embodies its contradictions.

Adepts: Ruhollah Khomeini (Iran),46 Recep Tayyip Erdoğan (Turkey),47 King Faisal of Saudi Arabia (OIC founder),48 Ayatollah Ali Khamenei,49 and Prince Turki al-Faisal (Saudi intelligence/diplomat).50

10a) Revolutionary Confrontational Axis (Iran-anchored)

Vector: (+2, –3, +9)

Why: Frames Palestine through anti-colonial resistance; prioritizes deterrence over calm; legal-justice language appears, but practice is leverage-first.

Adepts: Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Hassan Nasrallah, Qassem Soleimani / Esmail Qaani (IRGC-QF), Abdul-Malik al-Houthi, Ziad al-Nakhala (PIJ).

10b) Turkish Competitive Mediation (AKP/neo-Ottoman)

Vector: (+3, +6, +4)

Why: High-visibility mediation and relief; justice rhetoric present; maintains channels with all sides; tolerates symbolic resistance while seeking regional stability.

Adepts: Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, Hakan Fidan, Ahmet Davutoğlu, İbrahim Kalın, Mevlüt Çavuşoğlu.

10c) Qatari Platforming & Shuttle Mediation

Vector: (+5, +7, +3)

Why: Hosts/pivots talks, bankrolls relief/reconstruction, sustains media narrative (AJ); pushes stability via deals while keeping justice discourse alive; limited embrace of armed leverage.

Adepts: Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani, Mutlaq al-Qahtani, Khalid al-Attiyah, Al Jazeera network (institutional platform).

10d) Custodial Monarchic Diplomacy (Holy Sites / Status-Quo Stewardship)

Vector: (+1, +8, –6)

Why: Prioritizes regime/region stability, de-escalation, custodianship optics (Jerusalem/Haramain); justice claims moderated; opposes disruptive resistance.

Adepts: King Abdullah II & Ayman Safadi (Jordan), King Mohammed VI (Morocco), Saudi FM Prince Faisal bin Farhan, Mohammed bin Salman (in this track’s mode).

Note: This is distinct from your separate “Gulf–Normalization Pragmatism” flavor (UAE/Bahrain/Abraham Accords). Saudi can oscillate: custodial status-quo here vs. normalization (#6).

10e) Islamist Parliamentary/Movemental Pragmatism (non-violent first)

Vector: (+6, +3, +5)

Why: Seeks rights via elections, law, social mobilization; accepts calibrated leverage under occupation; stability secondary to dignity but not dismissed.

Adepts: Rached Ghannouchi (Ennahda), Ismail Haniyeh / Khaled Meshaal (political bureau track), Abdel Moneim Aboul Fotouh, Yusuf al-Qaradawi (intellectual lineage).

10f) OIC Multilateral Legalism (institutional lane)

Vector: (+7, +6, +1)

Why: Runs on resolutions, ICJ/UN referrals, boycotts/sanctions discourse; emphasizes law + collective stability; keeps armed resistance out of frame.

Adepts: Hissein Brahim Taha (OIC SG), Iyad Madani (former SG), Retno Marsudi (Indonesia FM), Anwar Ibrahim / Mahathir Mohamad (Malaysia), Imran Khan (Pakistan, 2019–22).

11. Global South Pragmatism

For the Global South, Palestine symbolizes unfinished decolonization. Its origins lie in Bandung (1955) and the Non-Aligned Movement. Lawfare at the UN is its main weapon.

Adepts: Nelson Mandela,51 Fidel Castro,52 Evo Morales,53 Julius Nyerere,54 and Desmond Tutu.55

12. Humanitarian Pragmatism

This current saves lives but not futures. Its creed: power the NICU today, fight politics tomorrow. Its genealogy goes back to Dunant’s Red Cross.

Adepts: Peter Maurer (ICRC),56 Philippe Lazzarini (UNRWA Commissioner-General),57 Jan Egeland (Norwegian Refugee Council),58 James Orbinski (MSF president during Rwanda),59 and António Guterres (UNHCR, pre-SG).60

13. Jewish–Israeli Pragmatism

Fractured between annexationists and peace-camp remnants, Israeli pragmatism has swung over time. Ben-Gurion once balanced vision with realpolitik; today’s leaders use “security” to justify annexation.

Adepts: David Ben-Gurion,61 Yitzhak Rabin,62 Ariel Sharon,63 Benjamin Netanyahu,64 and Yair Lapid.65

13a) State-Security Zionism (mainstream/centrist)

  • Philosophy: Preserve Israel’s security architecture; manage the conflict; incrementalism.
  • Examples: David Ben-Gurion, Yitzhak Rabin, Benny Gantz, Yair Lapid, Gadi Eisenkot.
  • Vector (X=Justice, Y=Stability, Z=Resistance): (−5, +9, −7)
    • Justice −5: rights subordinated to security imperatives.
    • Stability +9: core priority (deterrence, calm, coordination).
    • Resistance −7: suppresses Palestinian resistance (armed or disruptive).

13b) Religious-Zionist Annexationism (settler movement / maximalists)

  • Philosophy: Sovereignty/annexation over occupied land; messianic-national ethos.
  • Examples: Bezalel Smotrich, Itamar Ben-Gvir, Naftali Bennett, Religious Zionism party, Yesha Council.
  • Vector: (−9, +8, −9)
    • Justice −9: rejects international legal constraints on settlements/annexation.
    • Stability +8: seeks “order” via forceful control.
    • Resistance −9: crushes Palestinian resistance; views concessions as capitulation.

13c) Haredi Communal Pragmatism (non-/ambivalent-Zionist, coalition bargaining)

  • Philosophy: Prioritize communal autonomy (education, exemptions, welfare); transactional with the state.
  • Examples: Aryeh Deri (Shas), Moshe Gafni (UTJ), Yaakov Litzman, Agudat Yisrael, major Haredi rabbinic courts.
  • Vector: (−2, +8, −6)
    • Justice −2: Palestinian rights rarely central; sometimes restraining on provocative moves, often silent.
    • Stability +8: high—coalition deals, budgets, status quo.
    • Resistance −6: opposes Palestinian resistance; avoids confrontation unless community interests touched.

13d) Jewish Anti-Occupation / Progressive Dissent (Israel & diaspora)

  • Philosophy: End occupation and equal rights via law, nonviolence, and public dissent.
  • Examples: Hagai El-Ad (B’Tselem), Yehuda Shaul (Breaking the Silence), Peter Beinart, Jewish Voice for Peace (JVP), IfNotNow.
  • Vector: (+8, +3, +2)
    • Justice +8: centers IHL/HRL and equality.
    • Stability +3: accepts calm when tied to rights; rejects “calm” that freezes injustice.
    • Resistance +2: supports civil/nonviolent resistance; rejects attacks on civilians.

Note: Orthodox ≠ Religious-Zionist. Many Haredi groups are non-Zionist or anti-Zionist yet pragmatic with the state; a small fringe (e.g., Neturei Karta) engages in anti-Zionist activism—if you want that separately, we can add: Orthodox Anti-Zionist Fringe (optional) → (+5, 0, +1) (justice language re: Palestinians varies; often symbolic, sometimes controversial alliances).

14. Western Progressive Pragmatism

Born of post-Vietnam disillusion, this current pushes Israel without rupture. It walks carefully between solidarity and fear of backlash.

Adepts: Bernie Sanders,66 Jeremy Corbyn,67 Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez,68 Jean-Luc Mélenchon,69 and Naomi Klein.70

15. Grassroots/Activist Pragmatism

Here lies boycott, divestment, and civil resistance. Its lineage is Gandhi’s satyagraha and South Africa’s BDS campaigns. It wagers that consumer choices and litigation can erode apartheid.

Adepts: Omar Barghouti (BDS co-founder),71 Angela Davis,72 Judith Butler,73 Roger Waters,74 and Cornel West.75

Conclusion

The fifteen flavors of pragmatism map not only a conflict but a world. Each current has its philosophers and adepts, its genealogy and creed. Together they reveal that “pragmatism” is no neutral realism: it is a contested grammar of survival, compromise, resistance, and control. To expose these currents is to distinguish effective remedies for injustice from symbolic moves that don’t change outcomes.
