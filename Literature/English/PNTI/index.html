The Principle of Non-Transfer of Injustice

Español · English · Français · Italiano · عربي
By Qais Aljoan — September 2025

Method note. This essay states an ethical axiom grounded in moral philosophy and positive international law. It rejects collective blame and calls for individual responsibility and remedies that never burden innocents.

I. Statement of the axiom

None of the world’s injustices can be repaired by offloading their costs onto innocents. Justice requires individualized responsibility: we hold perpetrators to account, not those who share their religion, ethnicity, or passport. Violating this rule doesn’t fix the original wrong; it renews it and multiplies it.

Ethical axiom: Guilt is not inherited, nor transmitted by blood or community. The only legitimate inheritance is memory as warning, and responsibility not to repeat.

II. Ethical and philosophical foundations

From Aristotle to Kant, moral thought judges action by its authorship. Kantian autonomy rejects imputing acts to those who did not commit them. In Islamic and Christian sources, “no soul bears another’s burden” (Qur’an 6:164; Ezekiel 18:20). In Buddhism, karma follows one’s own deeds; Confucian yi requires proportion without vicarious punishment; ubuntu rejects harming innocents as harming the whole; Māori/Mapuche notions of restoration aim to heal harmony, not perpetuate intergenerational vendettas.

Collective punishment denies the dignity of each person as a free and responsible subject. The axiom is universal.

III. Foundations in international law

  • Geneva Convention IV, art. 33 (1949): explicit ban on collective punishment.
  • Rome Statute (1998): international criminal responsibility is individual, not collective.
  • Legality (nulla poena sine culpa): no penalty without attributable conduct.
  • UDHR (1948): fair trial rights; no inherited condemnation.

IV. Historical cases of derived/transfer injustice

Shoah – Nakba: a European crime against Jews; Palestinians—uninvolved—were dispossessed as a vicariant “payment”.

Karbala – Sunna: Husayn’s martyrdom cannot justify enmity toward contemporary Sunni innocents.

Alawites in Syria: blaming an entire minority for regime crimes creates a new injustice; many Alawites suffered too.

Muslims after 9/11: acts by a small group; mass stigmatization and wars punished innocents.

Tutsi & Hutu (Rwanda 1994): guilt belongs to perpetrators; collective blame feeds cycles of hatred.

Bosnia & Serbs (1990s): international justice targeted leaders and commanders, not whole peoples.

Slavery & structural racism: no inherited guilt, but real duties of social justice to dismantle discrimination.

Japan post-1945: imperial atrocities do not brand future generations with permanent culpable identity.

South Africa post-apartheid: distinguish perpetrators from the broader society to avoid substituting one injustice for another.

V. The axiom and collective memory

Memory is legitimate when it universalizes pain: “Never again—for anyone.” It is perverted when it privatizes license: “Because it happened to us, we may do it.”
  • Karbala: warning against injustice in any era; not hereditary enmity.
  • Shoah: universal warning against genocide; not a blank check to occupy Palestine.
  • Nakba: memory to demand justice; not hereditary hatred.

VI. Conclusion

The non-transfer of injustice is a pillar of universal ethics and international law: guilt is not inherited; remedies cannot burden innocents; memory must vaccinate against repetition, not license new wrongs.

When a people uses its tragedy to justify the oppression of another, it betrays the memory of its own victims.
