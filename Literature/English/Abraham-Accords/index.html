Abraham Accords: The Counterfeit Product Wrapped in False Promises

By Qais Aljoan — 11 September 2025

The Packaging

When the Abraham Accords were unveiled in 2020, the images were carefully staged. Leaders shook hands beneath the White House portico, flags fluttered, and headlines proclaimed the dawn of a “new Middle East.” Normalization with Israel was sold as pragmatic: stability in exchange for recognition, prosperity in exchange for silence on Palestine.

The packaging was immaculate. The promises were clear: peace, protection, and progress. But like all counterfeits, the shine concealed a hollow core.

The Counterfeit

From the start, the Accords were built on false premises. They bypassed the central wound of the region — the dispossession of Palestine — and offered instead the illusion that peace can exist without justice.

They promised stability while occupation deepened. They promised security while exposing partners to risk. They promised prosperity while binding economies to political submission.

Peace is not a commodity, and sovereignty is not for sale. What the Accords offered was not protection but dependency. They mimicked the appearance of normal relations while eroding the substance of independence.

The Breaking Point: Doha

The Israeli strike on Doha exposed the counterfeit in brutal clarity. Qatar — wealthy, diplomatically active, and home to the largest American base in the region — was supposed to embody the stability that normalization promised. Yet a single jet pierced its skies.

This was no accident. Jets do not fly hundreds of miles without corridors cleared, radars muted, and fuel supplied. The trigger was Israeli, but the logistics spoke English. The message was unmistakable: sovereignty under the current order is conditional.

If normalization was meant to purchase safety, Doha proved the product defective. A counterfeit always fails its first real test.

The Real Cost

For Gulf states, the price of the Accords is now visible. Normalization did not deliver immunity from conflict; it imported vulnerability. By aligning with Israel, they tethered their sovereignty to a partner defined by perpetual confrontation.

The arrangement leaves them trapped. To distance themselves from Israel risks Western disfavor; to embrace Israel invites complicity in its aggression. Either path narrows sovereignty. The Accords, marketed as modern, revive an old pattern: protection in exchange for obedience. But this time, the protector has struck the protected.

The cost is also internal. Citizens were told that prosperity required silence on Palestine, that peace could be achieved without justice. Doha’s smoke has made such silence untenable. Populations now see normalization not as a shield but as a shackle.

Counterfeit Peace vs. True Peace

Counterfeit peace is more dangerous than open hostility. An enemy declares itself; a counterfeit lulls its victims into complacency, only to betray them later. The Abraham Accords created a false sense of security, numbing states into dependency. When betrayal came, it was total: sovereignty exposed, alliances revealed as traps, and the promise of safety shattered.

True peace cannot be built on exclusions and silences. It demands justice for Palestine, respect for sovereignty, and equal application of law. Anything less is performance, not stability.

Conclusion — The Wrapping is Torn

The Abraham Accords promised prosperity but delivered vulnerability. They promised safety but exposed capitals to fire. They promised modernity but revived old hierarchies.

The strike on Doha tore the wrapping. The counterfeit product stands revealed: a fragile arrangement dressed as progress, built on dependency and denial.

The lesson is stark. No amount of packaging can transform injustice into peace. No flag ceremony can disguise a counterfeit. And no promise, however polished, can substitute for sovereignty and justice.
